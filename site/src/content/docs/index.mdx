## Mid-flight, mid-sort.

Open mxr on a plane with the wifi off. Triage 200 emails. Write three replies. Draft tomorrow's announcement. Snooze the threads that aren't actionable yet.

When you land and your phone reconnects, mxr finishes what you started. Reads sync. Drafts queue. Replies send. Snoozes register. Nothing was waiting on a network round-trip while you worked.

Your archive is on disk, not in someone else's cloud. Years of email, every attachment, every label — local, indexed, yours.

Search the legal contract from 2019 by the supplier's name. Find the apology email you sent in March. Pull every receipt from a vendor. mxr ranks by BM25 with field boosts (subject, body, sender, attachment names) — instantly, no network involved.

```bash
mxr search "from:supplier subject:contract has:attachment after:2019"
mxr search "subject:invoice" --format json | jq '.[] | .from.email' | sort -u
mxr storage --by sender --limit 20      # who's eating your inbox?
mxr stale --mine --older-than-days 14   # threads waiting on a reply
mxr wrapped --year 2025                  # year in review, your data only
```

Type a query, see results. No spinner, no "loading more," no rate limits.

## When Gmail dies, you don't.

Google Reader. Inbox by Google. Hangouts. Stadia. Each one had millions of users when it shut down.

If your provider goes dark tomorrow — outage, lockout, deprecation, account flag — you lose a day or two of new mail. Everything else is on your laptop. You keep your archive, your search, your drafts, your filters. Re-point mxr at a different provider; carry on.

Your email is data. It belongs on a disk you own.

## The same engine, the surface you want.

mxr is a daemon plus clients. Three ship today; you can build a fourth.

<CardGrid stagger>
  <Card title="TUI" icon="laptop">
    Vim-native keys, Gmail-style chords. Six top-level screens including a full Analytics view. Compose in your `$EDITOR`. Drop into the mailbox without breaking flow.
  </Card>
  <Card title="CLI" icon="seti:powershell">
    Every TUI action has a CLI equivalent. `--format json`, `--dry-run`, `--search` for batch ops. Pipe through `jq`, `xargs`, anything Unix.
  </Card>
  <Card title="HTTP bridge" icon="random">
    Don't want a terminal? Run `mxr web`, point a browser at localhost. OpenAPI-described, WebSocket events, bearer-token auth. Use the bridge we ship — or build your own client against the same API.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Build your own" icon="puzzle">
    The daemon speaks JSON over a Unix socket and HTTP. Build a Raycast extension, a phone app, a Slack bot, an agent. Anything that can open a socket can drive your inbox.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Compose in your editor" icon="pencil">
    `$EDITOR` opens with markdown and YAML frontmatter. Your keybindings, your plugins, your muscle memory. mxr renders multipart and sends.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Distraction-free" icon="open-book">
    Reader mode strips trackers, banners, signatures, quoted text. You see what the email says, not what it's selling. One key to unsubscribe.

Works with **Gmail**, **IMAP**, and **SMTP** out of the box. One local database, one CLI, all your accounts.

```bash
mxr accounts add                    # connect Gmail or IMAP+SMTP
mxr sync                            # pull your archive locally
mxr                                  # open the TUI, you're in
```

## Open source. No telemetry.

mxr is MIT / Apache-2.0 dual-licensed. Built in Rust — one binary, no runtime. Nothing phones home.

We didn't invent terminal email. mutt taught us the keyboard belongs in mail. neomutt kept that work alive. aerc proved the terminal could host a modern MUA. himalaya proved a CLI mail client was worth shipping. mxr is the next step: a shared daemon underneath, so the TUI, the CLI, your scripts, your browser, and your agent all read from one source of truth.
