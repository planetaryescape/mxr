## Why mxr exists

Every time you open a web email client, you leave your flow. You type into a laggy textarea with none of the muscle memory you've built. No keybindings. No macros. No composability. Writing email feels like a chore because the tools make it one.

mutt solved this decades ago. It still works, but it feels like it. Configuration is arcane. The UX hasn't evolved. aerc is a step forward but doesn't reimagine the experience. himalaya is a CLI tool, not a full client.

**mxr sits in the gap** — a fast, modern terminal email client with the keyboard feel of a well-configured editor, instant search powered by a real engine, a CLI that makes every operation scriptable, and a local-first architecture that means your email is always yours.

## What makes mxr different

<CardGrid stagger>
  <Card title="Compose in your editor" icon="pencil">
    `$EDITOR` opens with markdown and YAML frontmatter. Your keybindings, your plugins, your muscle memory. mxr handles parsing, markdown-to-multipart conversion, and sending.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Search, don't browse" icon="magnifier">
    Tantivy gives you BM25-ranked, sub-second full-text search across your entire mailbox. Saved searches live in the sidebar as programmable lenses. Search is navigation, not a bolt-on filter.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Script everything" icon="seti:shell">
    Every single TUI action has a CLI equivalent. Pipe, chain, automate. Batch mutations with `--search`. Machine-readable output with `--format json`. Your AI coding agent can manage your inbox.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Daemon-backed" icon="setting">
    The daemon is the system. The TUI is just a thin client. Sync, indexing, rules, and snooze run in the background. Close the TUI — nothing stops. Build your own client if you want.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Local-first" icon="laptop">
    SQLite is the canonical store. Your email lives on your machine. The search index rebuilds from the database. Works offline. Cloud services are transports, not dependencies.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Distraction-free" icon="open-book">
    Reader mode strips tracking pixels, banners, signatures, and quoted text. You see what the email says, not what it's trying to sell you. One key to unsubscribe from anything.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

## Built to extend

<div class="extend-grid">
<div class="extend-card">
<h3>Build your own provider</h3>
<p>The adapter interface is a clean Rust trait. Gmail, IMAP, and SMTP are first-party. Outlook, Fastmail, ProtonMail — write an adapter and plug it in. The conformance test suite validates your implementation.</p>
</div>
<div class="extend-card">
<h3>Build your own client</h3>
<p>The daemon speaks JSON over a Unix socket. The TUI is just one thin client. Build a web dashboard, a mobile bridge, a Raycast extension — anything that can open a socket can be an mxr client.</p>
</div>
<div class="extend-card">
<h3>Automate with rules</h3>
<p>Deterministic rules engine with TOML definitions. Dry-run before you commit. Shell hooks as escape hatches. Inspect, replay, audit — rules are data, not opaque scripts.</p>
</div>
</div>

## Open source, built for contributors

mxr is MIT / Apache-2.0 dual-licensed. The entire codebase is open. No telemetry, no analytics, no "phone home." Your email stays on your machine.

The architecture is designed for contribution: clean crate boundaries, compile-time checked SQL, a conformance test suite for provider adapters, and thorough documentation. Whether it's a bug fix, a new provider adapter, a CLI improvement, or an idea — we want to hear from you.
