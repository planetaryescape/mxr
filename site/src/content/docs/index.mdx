macOS (Intel + Apple Silicon) and Linux x86_64.

[Download latest release ->](https://github.com/planetaryescape/mxr/releases/latest)

```bash
mxr accounts add        # connect Gmail or IMAP+SMTP
mxr sync                # pull your archive locally
mxr                      # open the TUI · you're in
```

Works with **Gmail**, **IMAP**, and **SMTP** out of the box. One local database, one binary, all your accounts.

Your archive is on disk, not in someone else's cloud. Years of email, every attachment, every label: local, indexed, yours.

Find a 2019 contract by the supplier's name. Pull every receipt from a vendor. List the apology email you sent in March. mxr indexes your mail with [Tantivy](https://github.com/quickwit-oss/tantivy) · the BM25 engine behind Quickwit · with field boosts on subject, body, sender, and attachment names. Optional local hybrid retrieval when you turn it on. No round trips, no rate limits, no "loading more."

```bash
mxr search "subject:invoice" --format json | jq '.[] | .from.email' | sort -u
mxr storage --by sender --limit 20      # who's eating your inbox?
mxr stale --mine --older-than-days 14   # threads waiting on a reply
mxr wrapped --year 2025                  # year in review, your data only
```

Every command emits JSON. Compose mxr with `jq`, `xargs`, `fzf`, your shell.

## Email your agent can fully operate.

Anything you can do with email, your agent can do. Not "compose a draft and stop" · actually run the workflow.

<div class="agent-conversation">
  <div class="agent-turn">
    <div class="agent-label agent-you">you →</div>
    <div class="agent-prompt">Find newsletters I haven't opened in three months. Show me the list, unsubscribe from the ones I confirm.</div>
    <div class="agent-runs">
      <code>mxr subscriptions --rank --format json</code>
      <code>mxr stale --within-days 90 --format ids</code>
    </div>
    <div class="agent-result">found 14 candidates. you confirmed 11. <span class="agent-ok">11 unsubscribed, 3 archived</span></div>
  </div>

  <div class="agent-turn agent-turn-flip">
    <div class="agent-label agent-you">you →</div>
    <div class="agent-prompt">I'm prepping for my 1-on-1 with Sarah. Pull all email threads between us from the last two weeks, summarise the open items, draft an agenda.</div>
    <div class="agent-runs">
      <code>mxr search "from:sarah OR to:sarah after:2026-04-23" --format json</code>
      <code>mxr compose --to sarah --draft</code>
    </div>
    <div class="agent-result">7 threads found, 4 with open asks. <span class="agent-ok">draft saved to ~/.mxr/drafts/agenda.md</span></div>
  </div>

  <div class="agent-turn">
    <div class="agent-label agent-you">you →</div>
    <div class="agent-prompt">Find every CI failure email from last week. Cross-reference with my recent commits and archive the ones whose tests have since been fixed.</div>
    <div class="agent-runs">
      <code>mxr search "from:ci@github.com after:2026-04-30" --format json</code>
      <code>git log --since=1.week --pretty=%H %s</code>
      <code>mxr archive --search "&lt;ids&gt;"</code>
    </div>
    <div class="agent-result">23 failures found, 18 since fixed. <span class="agent-ok">18 archived, 5 left for review</span></div>
  </div>
</div>

This works because mxr isn't a GUI with an API bolted on · the CLI **is** the interface. Every operation supports `--format json`, every mutation has a `--dry-run`, every search composes with `jq`, `xargs`, `fzf`, your shell. Your agent doesn't need browser automation, screen scraping, or a provider-specific SDK.

## Use it alongside Gmail. Or instead of it.

mxr is a client, not a replacement. It keeps a live two-way sync with your provider so changes flow both directions within seconds.

<div class="duplex-grid">
  <div class="duplex-side">
    <div class="duplex-side-label">in your other client</div>
    <ul class="duplex-actions">
      <li><span class="duplex-arrow">→</span> read a thread in Gmail web on your phone</li>
      <li><span class="duplex-arrow">→</span> apply a label in Apple Mail</li>
      <li><span class="duplex-arrow">→</span> archive a newsletter from your watch</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="duplex-bridge">
    <div class="duplex-mech">Gmail History API</div>
    <div class="duplex-mech-line">↕</div>
    <div class="duplex-mech">IMAP CONDSTORE + IDLE</div>
  </div>
  <div class="duplex-side">
    <div class="duplex-side-label">in mxr (within seconds)</div>
    <ul class="duplex-actions">
      <li><span class="duplex-arrow">←</span> mxr marks the thread read</li>
      <li><span class="duplex-arrow">←</span> the label appears in mxr's sidebar</li>
      <li><span class="duplex-arrow">←</span> the newsletter disappears from inbox</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

You're not locked in either direction. Stop using mxr tomorrow and your mail is still in Gmail / your IMAP server, exactly where it was. The local archive on disk is yours to keep.

## Open source. No telemetry.

mxr is MIT / Apache-2.0 dual-licensed. Built in Rust: one binary, no runtime, runs everywhere. No analytics, no phone-home, no third-party services in the hot path.

The architecture is designed for contribution: clean crate boundaries (`core`, `protocol`, `store`, `search`, `sync`, `daemon`, `tui`, `web`, provider adapters), compile-time-checked SQL via sqlx, a conformance test suite for provider adapters. Whether it's a bug fix, a new provider adapter, a CLI improvement, or an idea, we want to hear from you.
