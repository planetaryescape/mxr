## Mid-flight, mid-sort.

Open mxr on a plane with the wifi off. Triage two hundred emails. Write three replies. Draft tomorrow's announcement. Snooze the threads that aren't actionable yet.

When you land and your laptop reconnects, mxr finishes what you started. Reads sync. Drafts queue. Replies send. Snoozes register. Nothing was waiting on a network round-trip while you worked, because nothing ever does — opening a message is a SQLite read, not a fetch.

Your archive is on disk, not in someone else's cloud. Years of email, every attachment, every label — local, indexed, yours.

Find the legal contract from 2019 by the supplier's name. Pull the apology email you sent in March. List every receipt from a vendor. mxr indexes your mail with [Tantivy](https://github.com/quickwit-oss/tantivy) — the BM25 search engine behind Quickwit — with field boosts on subject, body, sender, and attachment names. Optional local hybrid/semantic retrieval when you turn it on. No round-trips, no rate limits, no "loading more."

```bash
mxr search "from:supplier subject:contract has:attachment after:2019"
mxr search "subject:invoice" --format json | jq '.[] | .from.email' | sort -u
mxr storage --by sender --limit 20      # who's eating your inbox?
mxr stale --mine --older-than-days 14   # threads waiting on a reply
mxr wrapped --year 2025                  # year in review, your data only
```

Type a query, see results.

## When Gmail dies, you don't.

Google Reader. Inbox by Google. Hangouts. Stadia. Each had millions of users when it shut down.

If your provider goes dark tomorrow — outage, lockout, deprecation, account flag — you lose a day or two of new mail. Everything else is on your laptop. You keep your archive, your search, your drafts, your filters. Re-point mxr at a different provider; carry on.

Your email is data. It belongs on a disk you own.

</div>

<div class="landing-section problem-section">

## Use it alongside Gmail. Or instead of it.

mxr is a client, not a replacement. It keeps a live two-way sync with your provider — Gmail's History API, IMAP CONDSTORE + IDLE — so changes flow both directions within seconds.

Read a thread in Gmail web on your phone? mxr marks it read on your laptop. Archive in mxr? It archives upstream. Apply a label in Apple Mail? It shows up in mxr's sidebar. Your existing clients still work; mxr is one more seat at the same table.

You're not locked in either direction. Stop using mxr tomorrow and your mail is still in Gmail / your IMAP server, exactly where it was. The local archive on disk is yours to keep.

## Built like a Unix tool.

<CardGrid stagger>
  <Card title="Daemon-backed" icon="setting">
    The daemon is the system. The TUI, CLI, and HTTP bridge are clients. Sync, indexing, rules, snoozing, and analytics self-healing all run in the background. Close the TUI — nothing stops.

## Don't like terminals? Use a different surface.

mxr is a daemon plus clients. Three ship today.

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="TUI" icon="laptop">
    Vim-native keys, Gmail-style chords. Six top-level screens including a full Analytics view (storage, contacts, stale, response-time, subscriptions, wrapped). Compose in `$EDITOR`. Ship as the default `mxr` command.
  </Card>
  <Card title="CLI" icon="seti:powershell">
    Every TUI action has a CLI equivalent. `mxr search`, `mxr archive`, `mxr compose`, `mxr storage`, `mxr stale`, `mxr wrapped`. Pipe through `jq`, `xargs`, anything Unix.
  </Card>
  <Card title="HTTP bridge" icon="random">
    `mxr web` exposes the daemon over HTTP + WebSocket. Point a browser at localhost. OpenAPI-described, bearer-token auth. Use the bridge we ship — or generate a typed client and build your own.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

The fourth surface is whatever you build. The daemon speaks length-delimited JSON over a Unix socket and HTTP/WS. Anything that can open a socket can drive your inbox: Raycast extension, phone app, Slack bot, custom dashboard. Your agent can write the client.

## Open source. No telemetry.

mxr is MIT / Apache-2.0 dual-licensed. Built in Rust — one binary, no runtime, runs everywhere. No analytics, no phone-home, no third-party services in the hot path.

The architecture is designed for contribution: clean crate boundaries (`core`, `protocol`, `store`, `search`, `sync`, `daemon`, `tui`, `web`, provider adapters), compile-time-checked SQL via sqlx, a conformance test suite for provider adapters, and thorough documentation. Whether it's a bug fix, a new provider adapter, a CLI improvement, or an idea — we want to hear from you.
